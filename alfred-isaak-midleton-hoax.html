Alfred Isaac Midleton: The greatest explorer that never existed.

January 8, 2023 by Fredrik

You might have seen this story on Instagram, Facebook, TikTok, Twitter, or any other social media site. It's a post accompanied by several old-timey-looking pictures and a text telling of the great explorer Alfred Isaac Midleton going something like this:

"British explorer Alfred Isaac Midleton (Or Middleton) traveled the farthest reaches of the globe during the late 19th century in search of zoological, botanical, and archeological wonders. These recently unearthed photographs help to shed light on some of his amazing discoveries during several missions to then-uncharted regions of Southeast Asia, Africa, and the Amazon rainforest. 

Unfortunately, all of his journals and scientific writings were lost in 1901 when Midleton and his team vanished during a Sumatran expedition to uncover the fabled Lost City of Dawleetoo. Due to these tragic events, scant details are known about what you see in these fascinating photos."

The character's name is a clue to the story's origin, A.I. Middleton. Add the city Dawleetoo, a play on Dall-e II, and the story's source looks quite straightforward. The author has been quite clever when constructing the narrative. Middleton was the name of an archaeologist and museum director during 1800; his full name was John Henry Middleton. An archaeological society was named after him in 2011. That the name resembles a real person makes the story more believable. 

Add then that the main story is based on the life of Percy Fawcett, who got lost when searching for a lost city in Brazil. The location of which is described in a document is usually referred to as Manuscript 512. This document of dubious origin describes the journey of a couple of slavers in early colonial Brazil. One day one of the unnamed slavers stumbled across a city with what looked like Roman triumphant arches and obelisks. The group didn't find much except one gold coin in the city. Several attempts to find this anachronistic roman looking town have been made, and today, most think this story to be a fabrication.

But the manuscript has inspired other works of fiction, maybe most notably Sir Arthur Conan Doyle's novel "The lost city." Doyle also appears in some of the stories about A.I. Middleton as the curator of Middleton's account. I also think it might be good to highlight that Percy Fawcett might be the inspiration for Indiana Jones. Note the resemblance between Fawcett and this picture claimed to be of A.I. Middleton.

Sensationalistic blogs like "Lost Books" and "Mysteries unsolved" has also invented a source in the shape of a book they in the text refer to as "The Lost Casket of Dawleetoo." As you might suspect, this book is also a semi-true invention. The book is named "The lost casket" and was written by a French author Fortuné du Boisgobey, and published in 1881. It does not contain Middleton, gold statues, Dawleetoo, or anything connected to this story.

If we look at the pictures, we see artifacts typical of AI-generated images. These artifacts are most visible in the photographs depicting people. Even if AI has come a long way, it still struggles with rendering naturally-looking humans. We also see that most photos are based on depictions of space shuttles. 

As AI imaging becomes increasingly advanced, it becomes essential to analyze stories critically. Who is the source, and how does the story hold up when examined? Overall, the dangers of AI-generated images highlight the importance of being cautious when evaluating the authenticity and reliability of any photos we encounter and taking steps to protect ourselves from potential abuse or misuse of this technology.

Some will try to use it to spread misinformation, and we need to prepare ourselves for this. We should not forget this technology's usefulness and possibilities in our research and when trying to picture how an archaeological site looked, for example.
